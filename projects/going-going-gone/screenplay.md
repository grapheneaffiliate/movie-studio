# GOING. GOING. GONE.
*50-second period comedy — an auction in one lot, 1895*

## Logline
London's fastest-talking auctioneer meets London's fastest thief — and when
the lot vanishes mid-bid before forty witnesses, he does the only thing a
true professional can do: he auctions the crime scene. **Feeling: unflappable
showmanship.** The joke is that NOTHING — not even an impossible theft — can
slow this man down.

## Treatment
**Act 1 (0–16 s) — THE LOT.** A gaslit Mayfair auction house, 1895. MR.
PEMBROKE at the rostrum, MR. HALE his long-suffering clerk beside him. The
velvet comes off Lot 13: the Jeweled Raven of Marylebone, under a glass dome.
Pembroke opens fire — an auctioneer's chant so fast it should be illegal.
**Act 2 (16–28 s) — THE BIDDERS.** Gloved hands and ivory paddles rise in
pools of gaslight. At the back: a figure in an opera cape, face in shadow,
bidding with a silver-handled cane. Hale leans, squints, stops smiling.
**Act 3 (28–49 s) — THE TWIST.** Gaslights gutter. Fog curls across the
stage. The dome is intact — the raven inside it is GONE, replaced by a
calling card with a black wax seal. One beat. Pembroke — *delighted* —
pivots: "A genuine vanishing! One of one! Do I hear two hundred for the
empty plinth?" A long pause… and Hale, defeated by professional admiration,
raises his own paddle. Gavel. "SOLD! To my own clerk. Heaven help us both."

## Character bible
**MR. PEMBROKE** — the auctioneer (element `pembroke-auctioneer`)
- 60s, **swept-back white hair**, **full white goatee beard**, blue eyes,
  broad showman's smile; conducts with the gavel like a maestro
- wardrobe, never changes: **black Victorian frock coat with silk lapels,
  white wing-collar shirt, teal paisley silk cravat**
- voice: rapid-fire plummy auctioneer patter — Rupert (en), written as
  breathless run-ons so the TTS sprints

**MR. HALE** — the clerk/spotter (element `hale-clerk`)
- late 30s, **short dark-brown side-parted hair**, square jaw; the straight
  man — his face does the slow realization the script needs
- wardrobe, never changes: **charcoal Victorian frock coat, white
  wing-collar shirt, burgundy striped silk cravat**
- voice: dry, measured — Callum (en); three short lines

**THE BIDDER** — opera cape, top hat, face never resolved, one
**silver-handled ebony cane**. Pure Moriarty perfume; never closer than 20 m.
THE AUDIENCE — never faces: white-gloved hands, ivory paddles, top-hatted
silhouettes in dark bokeh (generatability law: no crowds).

## Audio plan
- **m01_waltz** 0→28 s: elegant Victorian string-quartet waltz with harpsichord, sly and playful, subtly accelerating with the chant, stops DEAD on a gasp.
- **m02_mystery** 28→49 s: Sherlock-style pizzicato mystery strings, mischievous, tiptoeing, two final plucked notes at the very end.
- **d01_pembroke** at 8.6 s (Rupert): "Lot thirteen, the Jeweled Raven of Marylebone! Fifty pounds — fifty, do I hear sixty, sixty from the colonel, seventy, eighty, don't blink madam you'll cost yourself a fortune, ninety at the back, one hundred from the gentleman with the silver cane—"
- **d02_hale** at 25.6 s (Callum): "Sir. The raven is gone."
- **d03_pembroke** at 31.6 s (Rupert): "A genuine vanishing, one of one, the empty plinth, provenance: stolen this very instant before forty witnesses — do I hear two hundred?!"
- **d04_hale** at 39.8 s (Callum): "…Two hundred."
- **d05_pembroke** at 42.6 s (Rupert): "Sold! To my own clerk. Heaven help us both."
- **x01_hall_amb** 0→49 s: refined murmur, fire crackle, settling chairs (low bed). **x02_velvet** at 8.2 s: velvet whoosh + jewel twinkle. **x03_gasp** at 28.1 s: collective gasp and worried murmur swell. **x04_gas_flutter** at 36 s: gas flames fluttering, eerie airy whoosh. **x05_gavel** at 42.2 s: one crisp wooden gavel CRACK.

## Why it makes sense (story logic ledger)
1. The chant is established BEFORE the theft, so the joke of him out-talking
   a catastrophe is set up, not sprung. 2. The thief is foreshadowed (s06
   cane bid) before the vanishing — the twist is planted. 3. The dome being
   INTACT is the Holmes-flavored impossibility. 4. Pembroke's pivot is in
   character: a man who narrates value cannot stop. 5. Hale bidding is the
   ironic payoff of his OWN line — he announced the theft, so he buys it:
   the clerk's revenge on a lifetime of fast talk is to own the one lot his
   master couldn't protect.
