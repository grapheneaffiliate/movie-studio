# POWDER RIVER
*50-second western — Tombstone-style standoff*

## Logline
A killer rides into a played-out cattle town and calls out the one old marshal
who never hung up his guns — and learns, too late, why the graveyard behind the
church is full of men who were faster than him. **Feeling: cold reckoning.**
The western engine isn't the shot — it's the long, dreadful quiet *before* it,
where two men already know how it ends.

## Treatment
**Act 1 (0–16 s) — THE TOWN.** Sun-blasted dust and warped clapboard. A bell
tolls. MARSHAL CULLEN pushes through the saloon's batwing doors — long black
duster, tin star, twin Colts, a grey handlebar mustache and eyes like flint.
He steps into the street and the town empties around him.
**Act 2 (16–34 s) — THE STANDOFF.** At the far end of the street: a black-hatted
killer, faceless in the glare. The town holds its breath — a shutter bangs shut,
a horse is hauled away, a dog bolts. Tight on Cullen: the brim shadow across his
eyes, the slow flex of gun-hand fingers, a bead of sweat, the killer's coat
tail stirring in the hot wind. Spurs. Silence.
**Act 3 (34–49 s) — THE DRAW.** A heartbeat — both hands blur. One muzzle flash,
one bloom of powder smoke. The killer's silhouette folds into the backlit dust
and falls. Cullen stands alone, smoke curling from one barrel. He holsters,
tips his hat brim down, and walks back through the haze. V.O., gravel: "Should
've stayed on your horse." Title over the empty golden street.
